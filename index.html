How I think

Simple rules beat fancy words.

If an idea can't be explained clearly, it's probably not ready to build.

We serve burgers, not hot dogs or tacos.

Pick the lane. Know the customer. Solve the actual problem — not the adjacent one that feels easier.

A tool is only useful if people use it.

The fanciest system in the world is useless if everyone avoids it. Adoption is part of the product.

More software is not always the answer.

Fix the process first, then add tooling only where it clearly removes friction. Don't automate a bad workflow.

Build for the person who'll maintain it.

Simple systems outlast clever ones. The best build is the one that still works two years after you leave.

Diagnose before you prescribe.

The problem a client describes is rarely the root cause. Ask "why" one more time than feels comfortable.

Perspective

Don't reinvent the wheel. Just make a better wheel.

They need the broken parts fixed.

The wheel already works. Maybe it's slow, unstable, or hard to steer. That's where good building starts.

Don't throw out the whole system just to show off. Fix the pain, then scale what works.

01

Understand what's broken

Map the real workflow before touching any tools. Know where time actually leaks.

02

Fix the smallest useful thing

Ship something that works in days, not months. Iterate from real usage, not assumptions.

03

Scale what holds up

Only grow a system after it proves itself. Most things need refinement, not replacement.

FAQ

Common questions. Straight answers.

Clear expectations before we start.

What types of projects do you take on?

Projects that save time, reduce busywork, or solve real problems. Automation, dashboards, AI integrations, and custom apps for teams that need practical systems.

Do you work with small businesses or startups?

Yes. Most projects are with teams that need practical systems without enterprise complexity or a six-month timeline.

How is this different from hiring a developer?

You get process and system thinking, not just code delivery. The goal is outcomes and fewer recurring headaches, not just features shipped.

What is your process?

Diagnose the bottleneck, map the workflow, build the smallest useful system, and iterate from real usage until it holds up.

How do you charge?

Project-based scopes with optional support blocks for ongoing improvements. No retainers unless the work actually requires it.

How long does a typical project take?

Most focused builds land in 2–6 weeks, not months, depending on scope and integration complexity. Bigger systems get phased.

Can you work on existing tools and systems?

Yes, and I often prefer it. I improve what already exists before suggesting net-new systems — less risk, faster value.

What do you need to get started?

A 30-minute call to understand the problem, some context on your current setup, and an honest description of what "better" looks like.
