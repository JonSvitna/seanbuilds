How I think.

Simple rules beat fancy words. If an idea cannot be explained clearly, it's probably not ready to build.

We serve burgers, not hot dogs or tacos.

Pick the lane. Know the customer. Solve the actual problem.

A tool is only useful if people use it.

The fanciest system in the world is useless if everyone avoids it.

More software is not always the answer.

Fix process first, then add tooling only where it clearly removes friction.

Building

Don't reinvent the wheel. Just make a better wheel.

A short note on building things people actually use.

They need the broken parts fixed.

The wheel already works. Maybe it is slow, unstable, or hard to steer. That is where good building starts.

Do not throw out the whole system just to show off. Fix the pain, then scale what works.

Common questions. Straight answers.

Clear expectations before we start.

What types of projects do you take on?

Projects that save time, reduce busywork, or solve real problems. Automation, dashboards, AI integrations, and custom apps.

Do you work with small businesses or startups?

Yes. Most projects are with teams that need practical systems without enterprise complexity.

How is this different from hiring a developer?

You get process + system thinking, not just code delivery. The goal is outcomes and fewer recurring headaches.

What is your process?

Diagnose the bottleneck, map the workflow, build the smallest useful system, and iterate from real usage.

How do you charge?

Project-based scopes with optional support blocks for ongoing improvements.

How long does a typical project take?

Most focused builds land in weeks, not months, depending on scope and integration complexity.

Can you work on existing tools and systems?

Yes. I often improve what already exists before suggesting net-new systems.

Have an annoying problem worth fixing?

Let's figure out if it needs a better process, a better tool, or both.
